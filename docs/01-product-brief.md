# 01 — Product Brief

## Product identity

**Name:** Chuhai Lens  
**Tagline:** See your brand through local eyes.  
**Product vision:** Any business entering a culturally unfamiliar foreign market  
**Build Week market module:** United States  
**Initial customer wedge:** China-based and other international founders entering the U.S.  
**Recommended Build Week track:** Work & Productivity

## One-sentence pitch

Chuhai Lens helps founders and brands evaluate whether their websites, product pages, brand identity, social content, and ads feel clear, credible, and culturally at home in a foreign target market, using AI-led visual and language analysis, market expectations, public evidence, and transparent synthetic-persona reactions.

The Build Week prototype uses the U.S. as its only enabled market and China-based founders as its clearest starting use case. That focus is a proof of the broader platform, not a permanent geographic restriction.

## Problem

New brands, solo founders, factory owners, exporters, and DTC teams often enter foreign markets without having lived there or having the budget for a local agency. Their materials can be technically correct yet feel subtly “off” at first glance:

- language reads as translated rather than written for the market;
- type, spacing, layout, photography, casting, or visual hierarchy signal a different audience;
- claims and promotions do not match local expectations;
- shipping, returns, sizing, payment, or customer-support details reduce trust;
- trends, humor, memes, or cultural references do not land;
- product pages omit proof or reassurance local shoppers expect.

These problems are difficult for the creator to see because familiarity hides them. Hiring local reviewers or agencies is often too slow or expensive at the earliest stage.

## Ethical position

Chuhai Lens is not an “origin-hiding” tool. A brand may proudly communicate its roots, present itself as global, or make origin less central to its positioning. The product evaluates whether the experience is coherent and appropriate for the chosen audience—not whether the brand successfully pretends to originate in the target market.

The report must distinguish:

- objective or verifiable expectations;
- common but non-universal market patterns;
- subjective aesthetic impressions; and
- hypotheses that should be validated with real customers.

## Primary users

1. A solo founder preparing a first launch in a culturally unfamiliar foreign market.
2. A factory owner moving from wholesale/OEM into a branded DTC business.
3. A small China-based ecommerce team without U.S. market specialists.
4. An established Chinese brand testing a new U.S. campaign or product page.
5. A business from any country entering a target market where its team lacks lived cultural familiarity.
6. A consultant or agency using the report as a structured first-pass audit.

The prototype should visually and verbally prioritize the first three groups, not large enterprises.

## User job

“Before I spend money launching or revising this asset, show me what could confuse, alienate, or reduce trust for my target-market audience—and tell me what to fix first.”

## Core value

- **Low friction:** a URL or image is enough to begin.
- **Multimodal:** it reviews what customers see, not only written copy.
- **Evidence-led:** recommendations point to the relevant part of the submitted asset.
- **Market-specific:** each market module should be opinionated and evidence-led rather than offering generic “global” advice; the first prototype implements the U.S. module.
- **Actionable:** it prioritizes fixes rather than producing a vague cultural essay.
- **Transparent:** it labels uncertainty and synthetic reactions.

## Prototype scope

The Build Week version supports these review types:

- brand identity;
- website or landing page;
- product detail page;
- social media profile or post;
- single-image ad;
- carousel ad; and
- short video ad, subject to size and duration limits.

It produces:

- a readiness score and dimension scores;
- first-scan impression;
- annotated evidence cards;
- strengths and risk areas;
- prioritized recommendations;
- competitor/public-conversation observations when available;
- a lightweight AI-visibility result; and
- short synthetic-persona quotes.

## Explicit non-goals

The prototype will not:

- edit the website or ad;
- generate a complete campaign;
- conduct interactive mock interviews;
- claim statistical consumer-research validity;
- offer legal, regulatory, tax, or customs advice;
- guarantee commercial success;
- analyze private competitor data; or
- determine whether a brand is “really American.”

## Differentiation

Generic AI critique tools usually give copy suggestions or broad design feedback. Chuhai Lens combines four layers in one report:

1. multimodal first-impression review;
2. target-market-specific commerce and cultural expectations;
3. external market evidence; and
4. explicitly fictional persona reactions.

The product’s defensible value is the structure, evidence discipline, and prioritization—not the claim that AI can perfectly simulate a market.

## Global platform direction

Future market modules may cover the United Kingdom, European countries, Japan, Southeast Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, China, or other destinations. Each module must define its own:

- language and tone expectations;
- visual/category patterns;
- commerce, logistics, payments, sizing, and support norms;
- public research sources;
- target-market persona contexts; and
- uncertainty and regional-diversity cautions.

The product must not treat one market's conventions as globally correct or merely translate the U.S. rubric.

## Prototype success criteria

The prototype succeeds if a first-time user can:

- understand the promise in under 15 seconds;
- start with only one URL or image;
- finish input in under two minutes;
- receive a coherent report without technical setup;
- see exactly which visual or copy element each major finding refers to;
- identify the top three actions within one minute of opening the report; and
- understand which findings are evidence, interpretation, or synthetic illustration.
