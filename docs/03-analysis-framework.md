# 03 — Analysis Framework

## Scoring philosophy

The score is a navigation device, not scientific truth. It helps users prioritize. Every score must be accompanied by observable evidence and a short explanation.

Use a 0–100 overall **Target-Market Readiness** score derived from six dimensions. In the Build Week prototype, the visible label is **U.S. Market Readiness** because the United States is the only implemented module. The system may mark a dimension “not enough evidence” instead of inventing a score.

| Dimension | Weight | What it covers |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| First-scan clarity and trust | 20% | Immediate comprehension, credibility signals, visual hierarchy, obvious friction |
| Language and tone | 20% | Naturalness, translation artifacts, claims, terminology, voice, cultural references |
| Visual and cultural fit | 20% | Photography, casting, art direction, fonts, layout, density, trends, symbolism |
| Commerce expectations | 20% | Price presentation, shipping, returns, sizing, payments, support, proof, policies |
| Positioning and differentiation | 10% | Audience fit, category cues, value proposition, competitor distinctiveness |
| Discoverability and AI visibility | 10% | Search/AI presence, consistent entity signals, explainability of the brand |

Weights may change by review type. For example, visual fit carries more weight for an image ad; commerce expectations carry more for a product page.

The six dimensions are a shared framework, not a universal U.S.-derived answer key. Each future target-market module must supply its own evidence sources, commerce expectations, audience contexts, and rubric guidance.

## Evidence levels

Every finding receives one evidence label:

- **Observed:** directly visible in the submitted asset.
- **Verified:** supported by a public source or an objective standard.
- **Pattern-based:** aligned with a common market convention but not universal.
- **Interpretive:** a subjective expert-style reading.
- **Synthetic:** generated from a fictional persona perspective.

## Analysis layers

### 1. First-scan review

Ask what a U.S. viewer might understand in the first five seconds:

- What is being sold?
- Who is it for?
- Why should it be trusted?
- What action is expected?
- What feels confusing, crowded, generic, dated, or unusually unfamiliar?

The report should describe the impression without insulting the creator or using “foreign-looking” as a catch-all judgment.

### 2. Language and tone

Check for:

- literal translation patterns;
- unnatural collocations or sentence rhythm;
- product/category terms unfamiliar to U.S. customers;
- inconsistent capitalization, punctuation, units, dates, or currency;
- overclaiming, vague superlatives, or trust-reducing phrasing;
- jokes, memes, slang, holidays, or references that may not travel;
- robotic or overly formal customer-service language; and
- mismatch between the intended positioning and the voice.

### 3. Visual and cultural fit

Check subjectively but explicitly for:

- font personality and readability;
- spacing, density, alignment, and hierarchy;
- color and category conventions;
- model casting, styling, poses, settings, and diversity;
- photography quality and authenticity;
- image retouching and compositing cues;
- iconography and symbols;
- mobile-first layout behavior;
- promotional badges, countdowns, pop-ups, and urgency cues; and
- whether the visual system supports the claimed price and positioning.

Do not turn “U.S. fit” into one aesthetic. Explain which audience segment or category convention a judgment assumes.

### 4. Commerce and logistics expectations

Check for observable trust factors such as:

- prices in USD and transparent discount logic;
- delivery range and origin clarity;
- returns, exchanges, refunds, and cancellation visibility;
- sizing units and conversion guidance;
- taxes/duties disclosure where relevant;
- familiar payment options;
- customer-service channel and expected response time;
- warranty or guarantee clarity;
- privacy, terms, and contact information;
- reviews, ratings, UGC, press, certifications, or other proof; and
- inventory, pre-order, or made-to-order expectations.

The report may identify missing information but must not provide legal conclusions.

### 5. Positioning and competitor evidence

When research is available:

- find two to four relevant U.S.-market competitors or reference brands;
- compare value proposition, proof, information hierarchy, and experience patterns;
- search public Reddit discussions and review sources for category frustrations or trust drivers;
- quote or paraphrase sparingly with links; and
- distinguish category evidence from claims about the submitted brand.

Do not copy competitor creative or treat popularity as proof of best practice.

### 6. Lightweight AI visibility

The prototype tests whether the brand is legible to AI/search systems, not whether it has a definitive “AI ranking.”

Possible checks:

- Can the brand/entity be identified from public results?
- Is the brand description consistent across visible sources?
- Are the category, product, audience, and differentiators easy to summarize?
- Does the site expose clear text and structured signals?
- Are there obvious name collisions or ambiguity?

Report the query date, observed sources, and limitations. Never imply a single model response represents all AI conversations.

### 7. Synthetic-persona reactions

Generate three to five short quotes as if included in the findings section of a focus-group report. Do not offer interactive interviews in this product.

Each quote includes:

- a concise fictional profile relevant to the intended audience;
- one natural-sounding reaction;
- the element that triggered the reaction; and
- a visible label: **Illustrative synthetic persona—not a real participant.**

Personas should differ on meaningful buying context, not stereotype ethnicity, gender, age, or geography. Quotes must be traceable to the submitted asset and must not invent product experience the persona could not observe.

## Recommendation priority

Every recommendation receives:

- **Priority:** Critical / High / Medium / Polish
- **Impact:** trust / clarity / conversion / positioning / accessibility / discoverability
- **Effort:** quick edit / design revision / operational change / needs validation
- **Evidence reference:** screenshot or source
- **Suggested direction:** what to change and why, without pretending there is only one correct execution
