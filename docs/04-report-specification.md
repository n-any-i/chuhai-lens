# 04 — Report Specification

## Report goal

The report should feel like a concise agency diagnostic that a founder can act on immediately. It should not look like an unedited model response.

## Report header

- Brand or project name
- Asset type
- Selected target market; United States in the prototype
- Analysis date
- Report language
- Submitted URL and/or asset names
- Limitation notice

## Section 1: Executive readout

- Overall readiness score, 0–100
- Readiness band: Early / Developing / Market-ready with revisions / Strong
- One-sentence verdict
- Evidence coverage: Limited / Moderate / Strong
- Top three strengths
- Top three actions

## Section 2: First five seconds

A short paragraph answering:

- What does the experience communicate immediately?
- What is clear?
- What creates doubt or friction?
- What audience and price position does it appear to target?

Include one overview screenshot or key frame.

## Section 3: Scorecard

Display the six analysis dimensions with:

- score or “not enough evidence”;
- short rationale;
- one strongest positive signal; and
- one largest risk.

## Section 4: Evidence cards

Each major finding is a card containing:

- title;
- evidence label;
- severity/priority;
- cropped screenshot, submitted image region, or video key frame;
- optional highlight box or marker;
- what was observed;
- why it may matter in the U.S. context;
- recommendation; and
- confidence/limitation.

### Visual-reference capability

The prototype should capture or accept screenshots, then crop the relevant region for each finding. A finding stores coordinates or a reference to the source frame so the report can show exactly what it discusses.

Implementation levels:

1. **Minimum:** full screenshot with numbered markers.
2. **Preferred prototype:** automatically generated crops around relevant regions.
3. **Later:** interactive click-to-highlight overlays and before/after mockups.

The image must never contain the API key, browser chrome with private information, or unrelated personal data.

## Section 5: Language and tone

- strongest lines to keep;
- phrases that read as translated, unclear, generic, or overclaimed;
- concise rewrite direction or example alternatives; and
- tone consistency assessment.

Avoid rewriting the entire site in the prototype.

## Section 6: Commerce confidence

Use a checklist with present / unclear / missing / not applicable for:

- shipping;
- returns;
- sizing;
- payments;
- contact/support;
- product proof;
- policies; and
- price/discount clarity.

## Section 7: Market evidence

- competitor/reference-brand observations;
- public customer frustrations or trust drivers;
- links and access date;
- explanation of how the evidence relates to the submitted asset; and
- explicit note when no reliable public evidence was found.

## Section 8: AI visibility snapshot

- entity identified: yes / partial / no;
- what the brand appears to be;
- source consistency;
- ambiguity or missing signals;
- simple improvements; and
- limitations of the test.

## Section 9: Synthetic persona panel

Three to five quotes, each formatted as:

> “Quote tied to something visible in the asset.”
>
> **Persona:** fictional profile and purchase context  
> **Signal:** what triggered the reaction  
> **Disclosure:** Illustrative synthetic persona—not a real participant.

Do not provide a chat or interview interface.

## Section 10: Action plan

Organize recommendations into:

- **Fix before launch**
- **Improve next**
- **Validate with real people**
- **Keep as-is**

End with a one-week action list containing no more than seven items.

## Report-language behavior

If Simplified Chinese is selected:

- translate the report prose and labels;
- retain original submitted copy in evidence;
- show suggested English copy in English with Chinese explanation; and
- keep source titles in their original language where useful.

The application interface and Build Week submission remain in English.

## Required disclaimers

- This report is directional AI-assisted analysis, not representative consumer research.
- Synthetic personas are fictional and illustrative.
- Cultural and aesthetic responses vary by audience and context.
- Commerce observations are not legal or regulatory advice.
- Public-source availability may change.
